Why Privacy Matters

Privacy isn't about hiding wrongdoing. It's about control — your control over information about yourself. When you lose privacy, you lose the ability to make decisions without being judged, manipulated, or discriminated against.

Your data is used to build profiles that predict your behavior, influence your political views, determine insurance rates, affect job applications, and target you with psychological advertising. You are the product, not the customer.

"Saying you don't care about privacy because you have nothing to hide is like saying you don't care about free speech because you have nothing to say." — Edward Snowden

Privacy protects everyone — journalists, activists, abuse survivors, LGBTQ+ individuals in hostile environments, and anyone who doesn't want corporations profiling their every move.

📊

What Data Is Collected About You

By websites and apps:

  • •Every page you visit, how long you stay, what you click
  • •Your IP address (reveals your approximate location and ISP)
  • •Browser fingerprint (screen size, fonts, plugins — uniquely identifies you)
  • •Search queries and browsing history
  • •Location data (GPS, WiFi triangulation)
  • •Purchase history and browsing habits

By social media:

  • •All posts, likes, messages (even deleted ones, stored on servers)
  • •Your social graph — who you know and how close you are
  • •Face recognition data (if you upload photos)
  • •Tracking pixels on third-party sites
Even if you've never had a Facebook account, Facebook builds "shadow profiles" on you using data from websites with Facebook Pixel and contacts uploaded by other users.

What to Avoid

Google Chrome sends browsing data to Google by default. Incognito/Private mode only hides history from people using your device — your ISP and websites still see your traffic.

Passwords & Accounts

Weak and reused passwords are the #1 cause of account compromises.

🔑
Use a Password Manager Essential
Bitwarden (free, open source) or KeePassXC (local-only)
Generates and stores unique 20+ character passwords for every site.
📱
Enable 2FA on Every Important Account Essential
Use an authenticator app (Aegis on Android, Raivo on iOS)
Even if your password is stolen, attackers can't get in without the second factor. Avoid SMS 2FA.
Most important accounts to secure with 2FA: Email (controls everything else), banking, social media, cloud storage, and your password manager.
💬

Secure Communication

📡
Signal Best Choice
End-to-end encrypted messaging and calls. Open source, audited.
The gold standard for private communication. Used by journalists, lawyers, and security researchers.
✉️
ProtonMail or Tutanota
End-to-end encrypted email providers
Regular email is like a postcard — anyone can read it.

What about WhatsApp? WhatsApp uses Signal's encryption for messages but collects significant metadata and shares it with Meta. Signal is the superior choice.

💻

Device Security

  • ✓Enable full-disk encryption (FileVault on Mac, BitLocker on Windows)
  • ✓Use a strong screen lock PIN (6+ digits)
  • ✓Keep your OS and apps updated — most exploits target unpatched systems
  • ✓Review and revoke unnecessary app permissions
  • ✓Use a privacy-respecting DNS (Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 or NextDNS)
  • ✓Disable advertising IDs (Settings → Privacy → Advertising)
🌐

Network Privacy

VPNs Intermediate

A VPN encrypts your traffic and hides your activity from your ISP. But it shifts trust — your VPN provider can see your traffic instead. Choose carefully: Mullvad and ProtonVPN have independently audited no-logs policies.

VPN myths: VPNs don't make you anonymous. Websites can still track you via cookies, fingerprinting, and account logins. A VPN primarily protects you from ISP monitoring.

Public WiFi

Public WiFi is generally safe for HTTPS traffic. The main risks are evil twin attacks (fake hotspots) and unencrypted HTTP sites. Always verify you're on the real network.

The Threat Landscape

Cybersecurity threats come from many directions: opportunistic criminals running automated attacks, targeted attacks by skilled adversaries, malicious insiders, and nation-state actors. Most people only face the first category — automated opportunistic attacks.

The good news: most cyberattacks are not sophisticated. They exploit the same basic weaknesses: reused passwords, unpatched software, and clicking on malicious links.

The 80/20 rule of security: 80% of breaches are caused by just a few root causes — weak passwords, phishing, unpatched vulnerabilities, and misconfigured systems.
⚔️

Common Attack Types

🎣 Phishing
Tricking you into revealing credentials or installing malware via deceptive emails, SMS, or calls. Over 90% of data breaches start with phishing.
✓ Verify sender addresses • Don't click unexpected links • Use 2FA • Hover over links before clicking
🔐 Credential Stuffing
Attackers take email/password combinations from breached databases and try them on other services. Works because most people reuse passwords.
✓ Unique password for every site • Use a password manager • Enable 2FA • Check haveibeenpwned.com
💰 Ransomware
Malware that encrypts your files and demands payment to decrypt them. Often delivered via phishing or exploit kits.
✓ Regular offline backups (3-2-1 rule) • Don't open unexpected attachments • Keep systems patched
🕵️ Social Engineering
Manipulating people into taking actions or revealing information. Exploits trust, urgency, authority, and reciprocity.
✓ Verify requests through known channels • Never give credentials to inbound callers
🔓 Unpatched Vulnerabilities
Attackers exploit known security flaws in software that hasn't been updated. Eternal Blue (used in WannaCry) was patched months before it caused $4B in damage.
✓ Enable automatic updates • Patch within 48 hours of critical releases • Remove unused software
🏛️

Core Security Principles

🎯 Least Privilege
Grant users only the minimum access needed. If a process doesn't need admin rights, don't give them.
🛡️ Defense in Depth
Multiple layers of security. If one layer fails, others still protect. No single control is sufficient.
🔒 Zero Trust
Never trust, always verify. Assume breach. Don't trust network location — verify every access request.
📐 Attack Surface Reduction
Remove what you don't use. Fewer services, ports, users, and software = fewer ways to attack.
📋 Separation of Duties
No single person should have enough access to commit and conceal fraud. Split critical functions.
📊 Audit & Monitor
Log everything. You can't detect what you can't measure. Anomalies in logs often reveal breaches.
🎣

Recognizing Phishing

Email Red Flags

  • 🚩Sender email domain doesn't match the company (paypa1.com, amaz0n-support.net)
  • 🚩Generic greeting ("Dear Customer") when the company knows your name
  • 🚩Urgent language: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours"
  • 🚩Links that don't match the displayed text (hover before clicking)
  • 🚩Unexpected attachments, especially .exe, .zip, .docm, .xlsm
  • 🚩Requests for credentials, passwords, or payment info by email
Golden rule: If an email asks you to click a link to log in, don't use the link. Instead, type the website address directly into your browser or use your saved bookmark.
🦠

Malware Prevention

  • ✓Never open email attachments from unknown senders — scan with VirusTotal if unsure
  • ✓Only download software from official sources and app stores
  • ✓On Windows: enable Windows Defender (free, built-in, very effective)
  • ✓Disable macros in Office documents from the internet
  • ✓Run as a standard user, not as administrator
  • ✓Use an ad blocker — malvertising is a common malware delivery vector
  • ✓Verify software checksums (SHA-256 hash) when downloading sensitive tools
🔄

Updates & Patching

The majority of successful cyberattacks exploit vulnerabilities that have already been patched. Updates are not optional.

  • ✓Enable automatic updates for your operating system
  • ✓Keep browser and browser extensions updated
  • ✓Update apps promptly — especially anything internet-facing
  • ✓Replace end-of-life software that no longer receives security patches
  • ✓Update router firmware — often forgotten but critical
The WannaCry ransomware (2017) infected 230,000 computers using a vulnerability Microsoft had patched two months earlier. Patching is free. Ransomware recovery is not.
💾

Backups: Your Last Line of Defense

Backups protect against ransomware, hardware failure, accidental deletion, and theft. The 3-2-1 rule is the gold standard:

  • 3Keep 3 copies of your data (1 original + 2 backups)
  • 2Store on 2 different media types (e.g. internal drive + external drive)
  • 1Keep 1 copy offsite (cloud or physically separate location)
Critical: A backup connected to your computer can also be encrypted by ransomware. Keep at least one backup that is offline or air-gapped.

Test your backups periodically by actually restoring a file. A backup you've never tested is not a backup — it's a hope.
